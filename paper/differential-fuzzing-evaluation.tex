\begin{abstract}
We evaluate \textsc{cforge-fuzz}, a differential and metamorphic fuzzer
for quantum circuit tooling built on an \emph{amplitude} oracle:
equality of statevectors modulo a single global phase, rather than the
distribution-level oracle used by prior work (MorphQ, QDiff). The
amplitude oracle detects a class of gate-convention bug that is provably
invisible to every sampling benchmark (Quantum Volume, XEB, mirror
circuits, randomized benchmarking), established in the companion paper.
Running the tool against five production stacks --- Qiskit, tket,
PennyLane, Cirq, and the quantrs2 Rust simulator --- we find one new
soundness bug in the Qiskit transpiler, a regression introduced in
Qiskit 2.5.0 in which the \texttt{CommutativeCancellation} pass silently
deletes an \texttt{sxdg\,$\cdot$\,sxdg} pair equal to $X$, flipping
measurement outcomes at \texttt{optimization\_level}\,$\geq 2$. The same
class of optimization in the four other stacks handles the analogous
case correctly, across 56{,}000 random circuits. The main methodological
contribution is that a sound metamorphic oracle must \emph{quotient the
transformations the target is licensed to perform} --- global phase,
layout permutation, approximate synthesis, and finite numerical
precision --- and that the minimal shrunk witness \emph{names} which
quotient a false positive came from.
\end{abstract}

\section{The amplitude oracle and its hierarchy}

Given two statevectors $a, b \in \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$ produced by two
implementations of the same circuit, the fuzzer computes three
distances of decreasing strictness:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{N1 (amplitude)}
  $\;\min_{\varphi} \max_i \lvert e^{i\varphi} a_i - b_i \rvert$,
  minimized at $\varphi = -\arg\langle a\vert b\rangle$. This is the
  strongest notion of ``same circuit'' that ignores only an
  unobservable global phase.
\item \textbf{N2 (probability)}
  $\;\max_i \bigl\lvert \lvert a_i\rvert^2 - \lvert b_i\rvert^2 \bigr\rvert$.
  Everything any sampling benchmark can observe.
\item \textbf{N3 (observable)}
  $\;\max_q \lvert \langle Z_q\rangle_a - \langle Z_q\rangle_b\rvert$.
\end{itemize}

A divergence at N1 with agreement at N2 is, by the conjugation-invariance
theorem of the companion paper, invisible to Quantum Volume, XEB, mirror
circuits and randomized benchmarking. The quantrs2 v0.2.0 $R_z$
sign bug is exactly such a case: N1 distance $0.88$, N2 distance $0$.

\section{Targets and campaigns}

Each target is tested either differentially (two implementations of the
same semantics) or metamorphically (one implementation before and after
a semantics-preserving pass). All campaigns use a shared weighted
generator that over-samples the phase-family and discrete complex gates
($R_z$, $P$, $S$, $S^\dagger$, $\sqrt{X}$, $\sqrt{X}^\dagger$, $T$,
$T^\dagger$) at $5\times$, since convention bugs surface where a buggy
gate meets a correctly-implemented complex gate.

\section{Case study: \texttt{CommutativeCancellation} deletes
\texorpdfstring{$\sqrt{X}^\dagger\!\cdot\sqrt{X}^\dagger = X$}{sxdg sxdg = X}}

The metamorphic property under test is that the transpiler preserves the
circuit unitary modulo global phase. The oracle compares
$\mathrm{Op}(C)$ with $\mathrm{Op}\bigl(\mathrm{transpile}(C)\bigr)$,
undoing the transpiler's declared qubit permutation with
\texttt{Operator.from\_circuit} and disabling approximate synthesis with
\texttt{approximation\_degree=1.0} (\S5).

The shrinker reduces every hit to the same three-gate core:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.transpiler import PassManager
from qiskit.transpiler.passes import CommutativeCancellation
from qiskit.quantum_info import Operator

qc = QuantumCircuit(2)
qc.sxdg(1); qc.sx(0); qc.sxdg(1)

out = PassManager([CommutativeCancellation()]).run(qc)
print(out)                                 # only sx(0) remains
print(Operator(qc).equiv(Operator(out)))   # False
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
The two $\sqrt{X}^\dagger$ gates on qubit~1 are separated by a gate on
qubit~0 and are then cancelled as if they were an inverse pair. They are
not: $\sqrt{X}^\dagger \cdot \sqrt{X}^\dagger = R_x(-\pi) = X$ up to a
global phase, not the identity. At \texttt{optimization\_level}
$\geq 2$, where the pass runs, this flips the measured value of qubit~1
from $1$ to $0$ with certainty.

\paragraph{Root cause and scope.} The pass classifies gates into
cancellable families; in \texttt{commutative\_cancellation.py} the set
\texttt{\_x\_rotations} is \texttt{\{"x","rx","sx","sxdg"\}}. Two
identical $\sqrt{X}^\dagger$ gates are treated as inverses. Source
inspection across releases shows this set was \texttt{\{"x","rx"\}} from
Qiskit 1.4.0 through 2.4.0 and gained \texttt{sx}/\texttt{sxdg} in 2.5.0:
the bug is a regression in the current release. The adjacent case (two
$\sqrt{X}^\dagger$ with nothing between them) is folded correctly; the
fault requires an interleaved operation on another qubit. Reported
upstream as Qiskit issue \#16594.

\paragraph{Contrast.} The same class of pass in the other stacks handles
the analogue $\sqrt{X}\cdot\sqrt{X} = X$ correctly: PennyLane
\texttt{cancel\_inverses}, \texttt{merge\_rotations},
\texttt{single\_qubit\_fusion} ($20{,}000$ circuits) and tket
\texttt{RemoveRedundancies}, \texttt{CliffordSimp}, \texttt{SynthesiseTket}
($16{,}000$ circuits) are all clean. The bug is specific to the Qiskit
2.5.0 regression, not an inherent difficulty of the transformation.

\section{Methodological contribution: quotient the licensed
transformations}

A metamorphic oracle for an optimizer is only sound if it compares
circuits up to exactly the transformations the optimizer is
\emph{allowed} to make. Getting this wrong produces systematic false
positives that swamp the real bugs. We encountered four such quotients,
and in each case the shrunk minimal witness \emph{diagnosed} which one
was missing:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Licensed transformation & False-positive signature & Fix \\
\midrule
Global phase & any, $N1$ constant vs $N2{=}0$ & minimize over $\varphi$ \\
Layout permutation & witness contains a \texttt{swap} & \texttt{Operator.from\_circuit} \\
Approximate synthesis & lone small-angle gate & \texttt{approximation\_degree=1.0} \\
Numerical precision & uniform $N1\!\approx\!10^{-7}$ across all passes & force \texttt{complex128} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The four quotients. In the Qiskit transpiler, comparing raw
statevectors produced $119/3000$ false positives, every witness
containing a swap (layout). In Cirq, the default single-precision
simulator produced $80$ false positives with a telltale uniform
$N1\approx1.3\times10^{-7}$ --- float32 epsilon --- across every
transformer including the exact ones.}
\end{table}

The last row is the sharpest diagnostic: a real soundness bug in one
pass does not produce an \emph{identical} worst-case distance in an
unrelated pass. A uniform error floor across all passes is a property of
the harness (here, the simulator dtype), not of any target. This is the
concrete sense in which our approach differs from distribution-level
differential testing: by working at amplitude precision we must be
explicit about the numerical and algebraic gauge, and that explicitness
is what lets the tool separate a genuine $X$-dropping bug from a
$10^{-7}$ rounding artifact.

\section{Threats to validity}

The campaigns cover up to 5 qubits and 14 gates; deeper circuits may
exercise passes our generator underweights. ``Clean'' means no N1
divergence above $10^{-9}$ on the sampled distribution, not a proof of
correctness. The single confirmed bug is a modest count, but it is
consistent with the Csmith experience that mature tools yield few,
high-value bugs under differential testing; the contribution is the
oracle and the blindness result that explains \emph{why} this class was
missed, not a large bug tally.
